What Is Private Market Investing? A Beginner's Guide for Emerging Market Investors

Nilep Editorial • 8 Min Read • July 2026

If you have ever wondered why some investors seem to access opportunities that never appear on the stock exchange - opportunities in growing businesses, productive farmland, and high-yield assets - the answer usually comes down to one thing: private market investing.

This guide explains what private market investing is, how it works, why it is growing rapidly across emerging markets, and how you can get started.

What Is Private Market Investing?

Private market investing refers to the practice of investing in assets that are not listed or traded on public exchanges such as the Nigerian Stock Exchange, the London Stock Exchange, or the New York Stock Exchange.

When you buy shares through a brokerage app, you are investing in public markets. When you invest directly in a private business, a farm, a real estate project, or a private equity fund, you are investing in private markets.

The assets in private markets are not available to just anyone with a trading account. They are typically accessed through networks, platforms, fund managers, or direct relationships - which is why many people have never heard of them, despite the fact that some of the world's most significant wealth has been built through private market exposure.

Private market investing includes:

  • • Private equity - investing directly in privately held companies
  • • Private credit - lending to businesses outside the traditional banking system
  • • Real assets - farmland, infrastructure, and real estate held privately
  • • Agribusiness - direct investment in agricultural production, processing, and distribution
  • • Venture capital - early-stage investment in startups and growth businesses

Private Markets vs Public Markets - The Core Difference

Most people are familiar with public markets. You open an app, you buy a share of a listed company, and your investment moves up and down with the market every day.

Private markets work differently. Here is the most important distinction:

Public markets offer liquidity - you can sell your investment quickly. But that liquidity comes at a cost. Public markets are volatile, heavily influenced by sentiment, news cycles, and global events that have nothing to do with the underlying business you own a share of.

Private markets offer less liquidity - your capital is typically committed for a fixed period. But in return, you get access to assets that are insulated from daily market noise, often generate stronger returns over time, and are tied to real, tangible economic activity.

Research consistently shows that private markets, particularly in high-growth regions, outperform public markets over medium to long-term investment horizons. That is why institutional investors - pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, university endowments - allocate a significant portion of their portfolios to private assets.

The shift happening right now is that this access is widening. Platforms and groups operating in emerging markets are now making private market investing accessible to a broader range of investors, not just institutions.

Why Emerging Markets Are a Private Investment Opportunity

Emerging markets - Africa, the Middle East, Latin America, and Southeast Asia - represent some of the most compelling private investment environments in the world today, for several interconnected reasons.

Population and consumption growth.

Africa alone is projected to have the world's largest working-age population by 2035. That means more consumers, more businesses, more demand for food, services, and infrastructure.

Underpenetrated sectors.

In developed markets, almost every sector has been competed down to thin margins. In emerging markets, sectors like agriculture, logistics, digital commerce, and financial services still have vast room for growth and improvement.

Higher return potential.

Because risk premiums are higher in emerging markets, the returns on private investment - when properly structured and managed - can significantly exceed what is available in developed market equivalents.

Real asset backing.

Private investments in emerging markets are often tied to tangible assets - farmland, agricultural production, physical infrastructure - rather than speculative valuations. This provides a degree of security that purely financial instruments cannot.

What Types of Assets Can You Invest In Through Private Markets?

If you are new to private market investing, it helps to understand the range of assets available. In emerging markets, the most accessible and productive private asset categories include:

Agricultural assets - farmland ownership, crop investment, livestock farming, agribusiness operations. These are tangible, productive assets that generate income from food production and distribution.

Agribusiness portfolios - managed investments in agricultural businesses that cover the full value chain from production to market. These are typically managed by professional operators, meaning investors do not need to be farmers themselves.

Private business investment - co-investing alongside professional managers in vetted businesses across sectors like technology, retail, logistics, and services.

Alternative income assets - assets designed to generate regular income returns, such as structured agricultural investments with defined yield periods.

How Much Do You Need to Start?

One of the most common misconceptions about private market investing is that it requires enormous amounts of capital. While some private market vehicles are structured for institutional investors with large minimums, the landscape is changing.

Many platforms and groups operating in African and broader emerging markets now offer structured entry points that are accessible to individual investors, entrepreneurs, and diaspora professionals who want meaningful exposure to private market returns without needing the capital of a pension fund.

The key is finding a platform or partner that is transparent about how the investment works, what the expected returns are, what the investment period is, and how your capital is protected.

What to Look for in a Private Market Investment Partner

Not all private market investment platforms are equal. Before committing capital, consider the following:

Track record.

Does the platform or group have a demonstrable history of executing investments and delivering returns? Look for named partners, completed deals, and verifiable outcomes.

Transparency.

Can you clearly understand where your money goes, how it is managed, and how returns are generated? Opacity is a red flag.

Sector expertise.

Does the group have genuine operating capability in the sectors they are investing in? A group that manages agribusiness investments should have agricultural expertise - not just financial engineering.

Regulatory standing.

Is the investment platform operating within a recognised legal and regulatory framework? This matters enormously for protecting your capital.

Alignment of interests.

Does the investment manager have skin in the game? The best partnerships are those where the managing team has a direct financial interest in the performance of the assets they manage.

Getting Started

Private market investing is not complicated, but it requires intention. The steps are straightforward:

  1. Define your investment goals - income, growth, or both
  2. Determine your investment horizon - how long can you commit your capital?
  3. Understand your risk tolerance - private markets carry risk; make sure you understand the specific risks of each asset class
  4. Find a credible partner with genuine expertise in your target market and sector
  5. Start with a position that is meaningful but not overexposed - diversification applies to private markets too

The most important first step is education. The fact that you are reading this means you have already taken it.
